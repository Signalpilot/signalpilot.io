A Beginner’s Guide to Technical Indicators

Indicators are yardsticks, not crystal balls. Used well, they compress messy price/volume into a few actionable signals. Used poorly, they become a blinking Christmas tree that says “buy” and “sell” at the same time.

What an indicator is (and isn’t)

An indicator is a formula applied to market data (price, volume, time). It’s either an overlay (drawn on price—e.g., moving averages, VWAP) or an oscillator (separate panel—e.g., RSI, MACD). Some are mostly lagging (they summarize what already happened) and some are leading (they highlight extremes or momentum that tend to precede moves). Neither is “better.” The right tool depends on the job.

The core five (start here)

  1. Moving averages (SMA/EMA): Smooth trend and define pullbacks. A rising EMA‑50/100 with price above is a quick proxy for “up‑trend.”
  2. MACD: A momentum lens on top of trend. Crosses are less useful than slope and zero‑line behavior.
  3. RSI: Measures speed. Divergences can warn of fatigue; regimes shift when RSI spends more time above/below 50.
  4. Bollinger Bands / Keltner Channels: Simple volatility context—are we expanding (breakout) or compressing (squeeze)?
  5. Volume/OBV: Participation check—moves with weak volume are fragile; absorption spikes can mark reversals.
One job per tool. Ask: “What question does this answer?” Trend (MAs), momentum (MACD/RSI), volatility (bands), participation (volume/OBV). If two tools answer the same question, keep the cleaner one.

Combining without overfitting

  • Decide regime first. Are you trading with or against the current bias? Counter‑trend trades need stronger evidence.
  • Two confirmations are plenty. For example: trend up (EMA stack) + energy present (PVA/OBV) → time entry with RSI/SRSI.
  • Confirm on close. Intrabar signals wiggle; close‑confirmed logic reduces false positives and makes alerts reliable.
  • Keep timeframes consistent. Trade the timeframe you analyze. HTF assists can gate, not force, flips.

Common mistakes

  • Indicator soup. Ten indicators ≠ ten times smarter. It’s often ten times slower to act.
  • Ignoring structure. Indicators don’t cancel supply/demand or key anchored levels; they operate within that map.
  • No risk framework. Without fixed risk per trade, even perfect entries can bleed out via size drift and overtrading.

Putting it into practice (quick recipe)

  1. Set bias with a trend/momentum read (e.g., EMA stack + MACD slope).
  2. Verify participation (RVOL/OBV or a volume pulse).
  3. Mark structure (recent demand/supply, d/w/m anchors, VWAP centers).
  4. Time the entry with RSI/SRSI in direction of bias; avoid entries into nearby supply.
  5. Size the trade so 1R loss is tolerable. Let winners pay for exploration.

Educational only—never financial advice. Build habits, measure outcomes, adjust.
